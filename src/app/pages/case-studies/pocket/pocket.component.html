Pocket Website:
Designing a responsive website for a clothing retailer
Project Overview
ROLE
UX/UI Designer, Researcher
TOOLS
Sketch, InVision, OptimalSort
DURATION
5 weeks
Note: This was a speculative project for Designlab.
BACKGROUND
Pocket is a global clothing retailer with over 400 stores in 32 countries with a mission to offer clothing for everyone and sell quality clothes at a reasonable price.
GOAL
Design a responsive e-commerce website with a branding refresh that appeals to a variety of customers.
PROBLEM
Users have a difficult time navigating large product assortments, reviews, and sizing options when shopping online.
SOLUTION
Design an easy-to-use shopping experience with clear categories and a modern visual design that appeals to a wide audience.
How might we make it more convenient for users to find clothing items that fit their preferences?
Research
GOALS
  • Identify pain points and unmet needs of people when shopping online.

  • Learn what motivates people to shop online.
Sources: Statista E-commerce share of total global retail sales worldwide, 99 Firms M Commerce Statistics, CPC Strategy, KPMG 2017 Global Online Consumer Report
MARKET RESEARCH + COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS
I looked at market trends and what similar clothing brands are doing in the online shopping space.
  • 14.1% of retail purchases worldwide are eCommerce sales.
  • 49% of website traffic comes from mobile devices.
  • 51% of Americans have used a mobile device for online shopping.
  • 88% of shoppers think detailed product pages are crucial when making a purchase decision.
  • 55% of consumers search online for reviews and 47% visit the company website to research products.
I went through the online shopping experiences of similar clothing brands to spot common design patterns and areas to improve.

1-ON-1 INTERVIEWS
To better understand online shopping behavior, I interviewed three participants about an online shopping experience for clothes within the past week.
  • Users find many online reviews unreliable, dislike pop up windows that ask you to log in, multiple clicks to get to their desired product, and get overwhelmed by too many product choices.
  • Want free, fast shipping and easy returns for convenience, shop at specific stores that carry their unique size, and will pay a higher price for quality material.
  • Shop for specific items they plan to wear in the future, start the shopping process on their phone, and prefer online shopping in the comfort of their home.
  • Shop recommendations from friends and other recommended products on the product page.
  • Browse multiple retailers to compare, save items for later, and will wait up to a week before purchasing.
USER PERSONA
To understand who I was designing for, I used my research notes to develop a persona of the target user. Ashley prefers online shopping for convenience, relies on product reviews, and will sit on a purchase to ensure she is spending her hard-earned money on a quality product.

EMPATHY MAP
To help build the persona and understand the user, I categorized my notes based on the user’s surroundings. Getting into the user’s mind helped inform design decisions throughout the design process.

Define
PROBLEM
Users have a difficult time navigating large product assortments, reviews, and sizing options, so how might we make it more convenient to find items that fit their preferences?
SOLUTION
Provide a straightforward online shopping experience with clear categories and accurate product information.
PROJECT GOALS
Based on the original brief and research, I outlined the goals of the business and the user to find their shared goals and what key features to include in the design.

PRODUCT FEATURE ROADMAP
It was important to back up each product feature by research findings to prioritize our users’ needs when designing.

CARD SORTING
To construct the website’s information architecture, I needed to learn how people categorize clothing items. I asked ten participants to sort 30 clothing items using open card sorting via OptimalSort.
  • 69 categories were created, with a median of seven categories per participant.
  • Most popular categories were by product type: accessories, bottoms, coats/jackets, dresses, outwerwear, shoes, and tops.
  • Additional categories included: occasion, gender, season, and style/outfit.

SITEMAP
Using the categories from card sorting exercise, I mapped out the website pages. I included pages typically found on retail websites (Account, Help, and About) to provide users with support and company information to make informed purchase decisions.

USER FLOW
I made a flowchart to depict how a user would purchase a pair of jeans, which helped me decide what web pages to design so that the shopping process flows seamlessly for the user.

Test
OBJECTIVES
  • Discover unclear or missing elements that prevent users from easily finishing the task.

  • Test how easy the website is for users to navigate.
METHOD
Remote tests via Zoom
HIGH FIDELITY USABILITY TEST
I asked five participants who shopped online for clothes recently to complete the following task:
Find and purchase a pair of dark high rise straight jeans in a size 27.
All participants reported that the task was easy to complete.
AFFINITY MAP

I noticed an even number of successes and pain points, so there were aspects of the design that worked well for users in addition to areas of improvement.

A notable finding was how most users tried to use the sort by filters, but explained to me that they usually don’t use filters when shopping online. They only attempted to do so because of the specific task.


Improve
DESIGN REVISIONS
I made several edits to the website based on user feedback.

CATEGORY PAGE 2 (JEANS) + COLOR FILTER DROPDOWN
PAIN POINT
Wanted to use sort by, color, and size filters.

EDIT
Added color and size filters.
CATEGORY PAGE 2 (JEANS) + SIZE FILTER DROPDOWN
PAIN POINT
Didn’t notice the left side menu right away.

EDIT
Increased font size and weight on category pages to draw more attention to the menu to help guide users.
CONFIRMATION
PAIN POINTS
  • Tried to add item to bag without selecting color and size / tried to click size before color.
  • Didn’t notice dark color label because photo didn’t change.

PATTERN
Scrolled to the bottom of the product page to access detailed product reviews before purchase.

EDITS
  • Added a hover state to the Add to Bag button on the product page that tells the user to select a size first.
  • Removed “pick a color” prompt. Color will already be selected and labeled to correspond with default photo.
  • Added a scroll trigger that takes users directly to reviews section when they click on the rating or reviews near the top of the page.
Reflection
NEXT STEPS
Perform a second round of usability tests with the final prototype and make revisions before handing off designs for implementation.
WHAT I LEARNED

This was my first project where I applied the design process. I learned how important research is at the start because it impacts every step. I learned how to support my design decisions with real user insight.

To differentiate myself from the numerous e-commerce websites out there, I aimed to uncover distinct shopping behaviors during user interviews and testing to ideate solutions for an enjoyable shopping experience.
